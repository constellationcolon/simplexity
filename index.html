The (College Student) Diet Problem

Consider the canonical college student. After a hard afternoon's work of solving way too many partial differential equations, she emerges from her room to obtain sustenance for the day.

She has a choice between getting salads from freshgreen ($s$), or subs from BaconDel ($b$). She's a poor college student, so she will obviously want to get her money's worth. This is obviously an optimisation problem: she want to minimise the total cost she spends on food.

\[ \arg \underset{s,b}{\min} 12s + 7b \]

Well, it's not as simple as that. A girl's got to get her fill of daily nutrients. Fibres, protein, and carbohydrates are all important.

A salad from freshgreen provides a lot of fibre (5 servings), not so much protein (2 servings), and barely any carbohydrates (1 unit). A sub from BaconDel, on the other hand, provides a little bit of fibre (2 servings), quite a bit of protein (4 servings), and an sizable portion of carbohydrates (4 servings). The daily recommended servings for fibres, proteins, and carbohydrates are 4, 3, and 6 servings respectively.

subject to \begin{align} 5s + 2b &\geq 4 \\ 2s + 4b &\geq 3 \\ s + 4b &\geq 6 \end{align}

Linear Programming

The Diet Problem is a classic example of a problem in linear programming, whereby we are trying to optimise (i.e. maximise or minimise) a linear function, subject to linear constraints.
